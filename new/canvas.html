What is Canvas? Why redesign?

Canvas is a learning management system. For academic purposes, we use tools such as threaded discussion boards to discuss readings and assignments, Google Docs to co-create documents, chat and web conferencing to hold group meetings, and task managers such as Trello to delegate group work.

Learning Management Systems are also an unavoidable reality for today’s students. There are several competing products out there, but they all generally allow instructors to organize class materials and assignments and to view and manage student online participation.

Being students ourselves, we were already keenly aware of some of the pitfalls of the current utilization of these technologies. What we suffer most is the group and discussion part in Canvas. See picture 2&3 below

How about products? How about users?

In order to determine our initial approach to the design, we researched existing collaboration tools and the needs of our users.

Our research on student online collaboration identified autonomy and connectedness as two key factors that positively affect student participation. We also discovered that the technique of anchoring discussions in meaning produced superior results to typical discussion forum practices. These three concepts - autonomy, connectedness, and meaning - formed the philosophical basis for our design. From our competitive analysis, we identified collaboration tools present in most learning management systems.Our analysis of popular collaboration tools provided inspiration on how to improve resource sharing, co-creation of documents, project management, and asynchronous communication.

Furthermore, we conducted a survey with 33 students to determine their thoughts about using Canvas and other online tools to discuss and collaborate.

We conducted an in-depth interview with one UT professor and surveyed six more. Teachers shared their frustrations with the discussion forum and groups features in Canvas.

What to redesign?

From all of this research, we arrived at an initial conception of our project goals. We decided to create a redesign of Canvas that focused on collaboration tools - specifically the discussion forum and groups pages. We determined that our design should focus on the student experience, but that it should incorporate key features that were important to instructors and teaching assistants as well.

These goals informed the key design decisions taken to create our first low-fidelity prototype. To promote meaning-based organization, we created topical boards. We used tagging and quoting to create links between ideas. Our groups pages integrated the most requested features for project management. With all of these features, we focused on creating greater autonomy in students by increasing their choices and control in the collaboration process. For teachers, we concentrated on providing tools for interpreting students’ online collaborative activity.

How to design?

We went through two cycles of designing and testing with a paper-based prototype. User feedback from students, TA’s and instructors iteratively informed our design, the final stage of which we’ll show you now.
